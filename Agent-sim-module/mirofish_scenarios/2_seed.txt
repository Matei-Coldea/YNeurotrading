The U.S. Senate passed the RESTRICT Act on March 25, 2026, by a vote of 67-31, granting the Commerce Department sweeping authority to ban or impose conditions on technology transactions with "foreign adversary" nations. The bill, originally introduced in 2023 to address TikTok, was revived after intelligence officials testified that several popular AI assistant apps route user data through servers in China. Under the new law, the Commerce Secretary can unilaterally prohibit any tech product deemed a national security risk without judicial review.

Civil liberties groups including the ACLU and EFF condemned the bill as "the most dangerous internet censorship legislation in U.S. history," warning it could be used to ban VPNs, messaging apps, and open-source AI tools. The tech industry is divided: Meta and Google quietly supported the bill citing competitive advantages, while smaller companies and the open-source community fear it will be weaponized against them. TikTok issued a statement calling the law "unconstitutional overreach" and announced it will challenge it in federal court.

Senator Mark Warner (D-VA), the bill's lead sponsor, defended it as "long overdue protection for Americans' data," while Senator Rand Paul (R-KY) called it "a blank check for government censorship." President Biden is expected to sign the bill within days. Meanwhile, downloads of VPN apps surged 340% in the 48 hours following the vote, and the hashtag #RESTRICTact has been trending on X with over 2 million posts.
